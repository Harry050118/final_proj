**LLM-based Evaluation and Verification.** The use of large language models as evaluators has gained traction with the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm [1], where strong models are used to assess the quality of model outputs in open-ended settings, achieving over 80% agreement with human ratings. This approach has been extended to multimodal settings through MLLM-as-a-Judge [2], where vision-language models are tasked with scoring, comparing, and ranking multimodal outputs. However, LLM-based judges exhibit systematic biases including positional effects, phrasing sensitivity, and self-preference [1, 2], motivating bias-mitigation strategies such as criteria-order randomization and label rephrasing. Beyond evaluation, generative verifiers [3] reframe reward modeling as next-token prediction, enabling chain-of-thought reasoning [4] during verification, while self-consistency via majority voting [5] aggregates diverse reasoning paths to improve reliability at test time. Despite these advances, existing work has focused primarily on static text or multimodal outputs, and the use of MLLMs as verifiers of interactive agent behavior—where trajectories span multiple steps and modalities—remains comparatively underexplored. This motivates the target paper's systematic investigation of MLLM-based verifiers across diverse interactive environments and its identification of agreement bias as a critical failure mode.

**Autonomous Agents and Self-Improvement.** Language models have increasingly been deployed as agents that interleave reasoning and action in interactive environments. The ReAct paradigm [6] demonstrates that generating reasoning traces alongside actions improves grounding and interpretability in tasks such as web navigation and embodied interaction. Building on such architectures, Reflexion [7] introduces verbal reinforcement learning, where agents produce reflective textual summaries from environmental feedback and store them in episodic memory to guide subsequent attempts without model fine-tuning. These self-improvement pipelines critically depend on verifier quality: when a verifier produces false positives, agents receive no corrective signal for flawed behavior, limiting their capacity for self-correction. Similarly, in robotic manipulation, Diffusion Policy [8] learns visuomotor policies from demonstration data through conditional denoising diffusion, and its deployment in open-ended tasks would benefit from reliable verification of task completion. The challenge of obtaining scalable, accurate verification thus directly constrains the effectiveness of agent learning paradigms.

**Benchmarks for Multimodal Agent Evaluation.** Studying MLLM verifiers requires environments with diverse tasks and nuanced success criteria. WebArena [9] established a realistic self-hosted web environment spanning e-commerce, social forums, and content management, evaluating agents by functional correctness. VisualWebArena [10] extended this to multimodal settings with 910 visually grounded tasks and introduced Set-of-Marks prompting to simplify visual action grounding in web interfaces. OSWorld [11] further broadened the scope to real computer desktop environments with 369 tasks involving multi-application workflows across Ubuntu, Windows, and macOS. In robotics, the robomimic benchmark [12] provides long-horizon manipulation tasks and expert demonstration datasets, with tasks such as tool hang requiring multi-stage reasoning over physical interactions. Collectively, these benchmarks span web, desktop, and robotic domains, enabling evaluation of MLLM verifiers across diverse modalities and task complexities. However, their oracle evaluation scripts—typically hard-coded rules applied to subsets of environment states—can be lenient or brittle, motivating the target paper's investigation into MLLMs as more flexible alternatives for open-ended verification.
