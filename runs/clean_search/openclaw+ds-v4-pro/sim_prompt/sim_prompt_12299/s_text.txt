**VLM-based Mobile GUI Agents.** Driven by advances in vision-language models (VLMs), mobile GUI agents that autonomously interact with smartphone interfaces have attracted considerable research interest [1, 2, 3, 4]. The Android in the Wild (AITW) dataset [1] provided a large-scale benchmark with diverse real-world tasks and gesture-based interactions, establishing a foundation for training and evaluating device-control systems. CogAgent [2] demonstrated strong visual understanding of GUI screenshots through a specialized visual encoder and achieved state-of-the-art results on multiple GUI benchmarks. AppAgent [3] leveraged multimodal large language models (MLLMs) to operate smartphone applications through a flexible exploration-based framework. Auto-UI [4] introduced a chain-of-action mechanism that fuses historical action sequences with future action plans to enable screen-level interaction without relying on environment parsing.

More recent work has focused on open-source VLM-based GUI agents that approach the capabilities of commercial models. SeeClick [5] proposed GUI grounding pre-training to enhance LVLMs with explicit instruction-to-element localization, demonstrating that grounding accuracy directly correlates with downstream task success. UGround [6] introduced a universal visual grounding framework operating purely on pixel-level perception, constructing a large-scale grounding dataset and achieving strong results without text-based representations. OS-ATLAS [7] developed a foundation action model for cross-platform GUI grounding, leveraging a large-scale multi-platform corpus and unified action labeling to achieve strong out-of-distribution generalization. UI-TARS [8] pioneered a native GUI agent model that perceives screenshots directly, incorporating System-2 reasoning for multi-step planning and iterative online training for continuous self-improvement. Falcon-UI [9] proposed a decoupled training paradigm that first learns GUI context understanding from raw interaction sequences before instruction-following, demonstrating that deep GUI comprehension enables effective downstream task execution.

Several datasets and benchmarks have been proposed to facilitate mobile GUI agent research. AMEX [10] introduced multi-level annotations including interactive element grounding, functional descriptions, and stepwise action chains to support more robust agent training. AndroidControl [11] provided a large-scale dataset with both high-level and low-level instructions, enabling systematic study of in-domain and out-of-domain generalization for UI control agents.

**Chain-of-Thought Reasoning in GUI Tasks.** Reasoning plays a critical role in GUI agents, particularly for tasks requiring multi-step planning and context-aware decision-making. Android in the Zoo [12] introduced the Chain-of-Action-Thought (CoaT) paradigm, which explicitly models semantic reasoning in GUI navigation by capturing screen context, action thinking, target specification, and outcome prediction. The CoaT pattern has been shown to significantly improve performance on complex actions such as clicking and typing, particularly when combined with fine-tuning on the AITZ benchmark. The thinking - decision - grounding triplet underlying CoaT has been widely validated as an effective reasoning structure in subsequent GUI works.

However, the effectiveness of chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning for GUI agents remains nuanced. Li et al. [13] conducted an empirical study revealing that CoT reasoning yields inconsistent performance gains in mobile GUI agent tasks, with improvements observed in interactive environments but minimal or even negative effects on static benchmarks. Their findings suggest that reasoning benefits depend heavily on model architecture, task characteristics, and benchmark design. UI-TARS [8] incorporated System-2 reasoning through explicit task decomposition, reflection, and milestone recognition, demonstrating that structured multi-step planning can benefit GUI agents when integrated into the training pipeline.

**Self-Training and Preference Optimization for Reasoning.** Self-training has emerged as a key paradigm for improving language model reasoning capabilities. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [14] introduced a method for aligning language models with preferences without training a separate reward model, enabling efficient fine-tuning through pairwise comparison of preferred and dispreferred responses. Building on DPO, several works have explored preference optimization at finer granularities. Step-DPO [15] proposed treating individual reasoning steps as units for preference learning, achieving significant accuracy improvements on mathematical reasoning tasks. Meanwhile, DeepSeek-R1 [16] demonstrated that large-scale reinforcement learning with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) can incentivize strong reasoning capabilities in LLMs without supervised fine-tuning as a prerequisite.

In the GUI domain, self-training approaches have recently been adapted to improve agent reasoning. GUI-G1 [17] analyzed R1-Zero-like training for GUI grounding, identifying critical challenges including excessive reasoning degrading grounding performance due to image token overload, reward hacking in hit-based reward functions, and length biases in GRPO optimization. Their proposed Fast Thinking Template and box-size-constrained reward design achieved strong results with limited training data.

**Monte Carlo Tree Search for LLM Reasoning.** Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) has been extensively adapted from game-playing domains to enhance the reasoning capabilities of large language models. Tree of Thoughts [18] introduced a deliberate problem-solving framework that enables language models to explore multiple reasoning paths through tree-structured search, demonstrating significant improvements on tasks requiring planning and lookahead. Building on this paradigm, Xie et al. [19] proposed an iterative preference learning framework that uses MCTS to generate step-level preference signals from LLM-generated responses, enabling fine-grained policy updates via DPO. Their work demonstrated that breaking instance-level rewards into granular step-level feedback yields superior reasoning performance, and that on-policy data collection is essential for stable self-improvement.

ReST-MCTS* [20] developed a reinforcement learning framework that uses MCTS with process reward guidance to generate high-quality reasoning traces without manual per-step annotations. By inferring per-step rewards through tree search rollouts using oracle final answers, the method enables self-training with accurate value signals that improve both policy and reward model performance. However, such approaches typically require a process reward model (PRM) to evaluate individual reasoning steps, which demands large-scale manual annotation of intermediate reasoning - a challenge that is especially severe in the GUI domain, where step-level reward annotation requires real devices or simulators, making it significantly more costly than text-based tasks.

Chain of Preference Optimization (CPO) [21] leveraged the intrinsic preference structure of tree-of-thought search trees to construct pairwise preference data without external rewards or human annotations. By treating reasoning steps on the final search path as preferred and pruned steps as dispreferred, CPO trained LLMs via DPO to align with optimal reasoning trajectories, achieving performance comparable to tree search while maintaining low inference latency.

Our work, MobileIPL, differs from the above approaches in several key aspects. Unlike ReST-MCTS* [20], which requires a separately trained PRM, we construct a CoaT-tree through iterative sampling and use rule-based rewards with backward credit assignment to derive thinking-level preference pairs directly - without manual step annotation. Compared to CPO [21] and the MCTS-based framework of Xie et al. [19], which focus on text-based reasoning tasks, MobileIPL operates in the visually grounded mobile GUI domain, where reasoning steps involve multi-turn dialogues conditioned on real UI screenshots. Furthermore, unlike existing self-training methods that rely solely on final-answer correctness, our approach evaluates both the final action and the quality of intermediate reasoning steps, providing fine-grained optimization signals through Thinking-level DPO (T-DPO).
