## Related Work

**Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Agentic RAG.**
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) [1] addresses the limitations of static parametric knowledge in LLMs by incorporating retrieved external documents during generation. Early work established the one-step retrieve-and-read paradigm, but this approach proves insufficient for multi-step reasoning tasks that require dynamically adapting retrieval based on intermediate reasoning results. IRCoT [2] proposed interleaving retrieval with chain-of-thought reasoning, where each reasoning step informs the next retrieval and vice versa. Self-RAG [3] introduced trainable reflection tokens that enable a model to decide on-demand when to retrieve and to critique its own outputs, improving factual accuracy. More recently, Search-o1 [4] integrated an agentic search workflow into large reasoning models, triggering retrieval when uncertainty is detected during extended reasoning chains. These prompting-based methods demonstrate the value of multi-step, adaptive retrieval, but they rely on frozen model behaviors and do not optimize the underlying search decision-making process through training.

**Reinforcement Learning for Search Agents.**
DeepSeek-R1 [5] demonstrated that reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards can elicit sophisticated reasoning capabilities in LLMs, inspiring a line of work that applies RL to train models for autonomous search and retrieval. Search-R1 [6] extended this paradigm by training LLMs to interleave reasoning with real-time search engine queries, using retrieved token masking for stable training and an outcome-based reward. R1-Searcher [7] proposed a two-stage outcome-based RL approach that first incentivizes retrieval invocation and then refines knowledge integration. R1-Searcher++ [8] further incorporated a memory mechanism and a reward for internal knowledge utilization, enabling models to dynamically switch between parametric and retrieved knowledge. ReSearch [9] treated search queries as integral components of the reasoning chain trained via GRPO, demonstrating emergent self-correction behaviors. While these methods successfully teach LLMs when and how to search, they rely on coarse outcome-based rewards that evaluate only the final answer correctness. This approach does not provide direct feedback on the quality of individual search decisions, leaving the agent vulnerable to inefficient behaviors such as issuing redundant queries or neglecting necessary retrievals.

**Process Rewards and Search Efficiency Optimization.**
The idea of providing fine-grained, step-level feedback has been explored extensively in mathematical reasoning, where Lightman et al. [10] showed that process supervision significantly outperforms outcome supervision for training reliable reasoners. Extending this insight to agentic RAG, recent work has sought to improve search efficiency through more granular reward signals. Wu et al. [11] formally defined over-search and under-search as pervasive inefficiencies in agentic RAG systems and proposed β-GRPO, a confidence-threshold-based RL method that rewards high-certainty search decisions. DecEx-RAG [12] modeled agentic RAG as a Markov Decision Process with process-level supervision at the decision and execution stages, using pruning to improve data efficiency. ReasonRAG [13] introduced Monte Carlo Tree Search to construct process-level preference data with stepwise rewards for query generation, evidence extraction, and answer generation. ProRAG [14] developed a process-supervised RL framework with an MCTS-based Process Reward Model and a dual-granularity advantage mechanism. While these methods advance beyond outcome-only training, they typically rely on learned reward models, confidence heuristics, or constructed preference data, which may imperfectly capture the necessity of each individual search action. This motivates the development of methods that provide explicit, rule-based feedback on whether each step constitutes an over-search or an under-search, enabling more direct and reliable optimization of search behavior.
