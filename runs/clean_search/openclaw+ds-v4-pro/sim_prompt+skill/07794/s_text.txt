Related Work

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) was introduced by Lewis et al. [1] as a framework for grounding large language model (LLM) outputs in external knowledge, combining parametric memory with non-parametric retrieval from a vector index. While effective for single-turn knowledge-intensive tasks, this static retrieve-and-read paradigm proves insufficient for multi-step question answering, where what to retrieve depends on what has already been derived. To address this, Trivedi et al. [2] proposed IRCoT, which interleaves retrieval with chain-of-thought reasoning steps, using retrieved results to guide subsequent reasoning and vice versa. Extending the prompting-based paradigm further, Search-o1 [3] integrates an agentic search workflow into the reasoning process of large reasoning models, enabling dynamic retrieval at points of knowledge uncertainty and incorporating a document refinement module to filter noisy retrieved content. These prompting-based methods demonstrate the value of multi-turn retrieval but do not train the model's search decision mechanism, leaving the agent reliant on static prompting strategies.

Recent work has turned to reinforcement learning (RL) to train LLMs as search agents that autonomously decide when and what to retrieve. Search-R1 [4] extends RL for reasoning frameworks by training LLMs to generate search queries during step-by-step reasoning with multi-turn retrieval, using outcome-based rewards and retrieved-token masking for stable training. R1-Searcher [5] proposes a two-stage outcome-based RL approach that allows LLMs to invoke external search systems during reasoning without requiring process rewards or distillation from a cold start. R1-Searcher++ [6] further introduces dynamic knowledge acquisition, training models to adaptively balance internal and external knowledge sources through outcome-supervised exploration paired with internal knowledge utilization rewards. Addressing search efficiency more directly, Wu et al. [7] propose beta-GRPO, a method that formally quantifies over-search and under-search behaviors and incorporates confidence thresholds into the RL reward to encourage high-certainty search decisions. A survey by Lin et al. [10] provides a taxonomy of RL-based agentic search methods along functional roles, optimization strategies, and optimization scope. While these RL-based methods improve upon prompting approaches, they predominantly rely on outcome-based rewards or coarse confidence proxies that do not provide explicit, step-level feedback on individual retrieval decisions.

A parallel line of work has explored process supervision as an alternative to outcome-only rewards. Lightman et al. [8] demonstrated that process reward models, which provide feedback for each intermediate reasoning step, significantly outperform outcome supervision for training models on complex mathematical reasoning tasks. The DeepSeek-R1 framework [9] further showed that large-scale RL with group relative policy optimization (GRPO) can elicit sophisticated reasoning behaviors without heavy reliance on supervised fine-tuning, establishing RL as a viable post-training paradigm for LLMs. However, adapting process supervision to agentic retrieval presents unique challenges: parsing agent reasoning trajectories into discrete evaluable steps is non-trivial, and existing confidence-based or learned process reward approaches can misjudge when to search or introduce biases that only weakly align with true step quality. This motivates the development of training methodologies that can provide fine-grained, knowledge-grounded feedback on each search decision during RL training, addressing both over-search and under-search through explicit step-level evaluation of the reasoning process itself.
