\section{Related Work}

\textbf{LLMs and MLLMs as Evaluators.}
The use of large language models as evaluators has gained traction through the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm, where strong models such as GPT-4 are prompted to assess the quality of other models' outputs [1].
This approach has been adopted across text generation, preference learning, and open-ended evaluation, with studies identifying and proposing mitigations for systematic biases including position bias and verbosity preference [1].
Panickssery et al. [2] further identify a self-preference bias, where LLMs favor their own text generations over those produced by humans or other models, and show that self-recognition correlates with the strength of this bias.
These biases are distinct from the phenomenon studied in this work, which arises even when the verifier's inputs omit text directly generated by the evaluated agent and include multimodal observations grounded in environment data.
Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting [3] elicits intermediate reasoning steps to improve model performance on complex tasks, while self-consistency [4] aggregates diverse reasoning paths via majority voting.
Test-time scaling strategies [5] further show that allocating additional inference computation---through iterative revision or search against a verifier---can improve output quality.
However, as demonstrated in this work, these techniques do not resolve agreement bias: MLLM verifiers continue to over-validate flawed trajectories even when equipped with CoT reasoning, majority voting, and test-time compute scaling.

\textbf{Verifiers and Reward Models.}
Verifiers that assign rewards to agent behavior have powered key advances in AI.
In domains with well-defined success criteria, 0/1 rewards tied to game outcomes have guided self-play learning in Go, chess, and shogi [6], while formal verifiers have enabled progress in mathematical reasoning and code generation.
In more open-ended settings, learning reward functions from human feedback has been foundational: Christiano et al. [7] train reward predictors from human pairwise preferences over trajectory segments, and Ouyang et al. [8] scale this approach to align language models via reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF).
More recently, generative reward models (GenRM) [9] reframe verification as next-token prediction, enabling LLMs to produce chain-of-thought rationales before scoring candidate solutions.
Benchmarks such as RewardBench [10] and AgentRewardBench [11] have been introduced to systematically evaluate reward models, with the latter focusing specifically on multimodal agents across perception, planning, and safety dimensions.
These efforts underscore the growing interest in verifier quality, yet prior work has largely focused on text-based or narrowly scoped reward modeling.
This work extends the study of verifiers to open-ended multimodal agent trajectories, where success criteria are inherently ambiguous and verification demands nuanced reasoning over long sequences of visual and textual inputs.

\textbf{Autonomous Agents and Benchmarks.}
The development of autonomous agents that interact with realistic digital environments has been driven by several benchmark efforts.
WebArena [12] provides a self-hosted web environment with functional correctness-based evaluation across e-commerce, social forums, and content management domains.
VisualWebArena [13] extends this framework with visually grounded tasks requiring multimodal perception, introducing set-of-marks (SoM) prompting [14] to ground agent actions in visual interface elements.
OSWorld [15] benchmarks multimodal agents on open-ended desktop computer tasks across Ubuntu, Windows, and macOS, featuring multi-application workflows and execution-based evaluation.
These benchmarks expose fundamental challenges in agent capabilities, with state-of-the-art models achieving success rates far below human performance.
Agents in these environments typically adopt the ReAct paradigm [16], interleaving reasoning traces with environment actions to enable dynamic planning and grounded decision-making.
The evaluation of agent trajectories in such benchmarks has relied on scripted oracle verifiers with privileged access to environment state.
MLLM-based alternatives promise greater scalability and flexibility but introduce new failure modes, as explored in this work.

\textbf{Self-Improvement and Verifier-Guided Feedback.}
The quality of verifier outputs directly impacts downstream applications that rely on them.
In self-improvement, Reflexion [17] introduces a verbal reinforcement learning framework where language agents convert environmental feedback into self-reflective summaries stored in episodic memory, enabling iterative improvement without model fine-tuning.
The effectiveness of such pipelines hinges on the reliability of the underlying evaluation signal: when verifiers produce false positives, agents lack the corrective feedback needed for self-improvement.
Online supervision methods that use verifier rewards to steer agent behavior during execution are similarly sensitive to reward quality.
Prior work has demonstrated that reflexion-based refinement can yield performance gains across decision-making, reasoning, and programming tasks [17], yet these gains depend on access to accurate evaluation signals.
This work examines how verifier quality---and specifically agreement bias---affects self-improvement and online supervision in multimodal agent settings, where reliable evaluation is both critical and challenging to obtain.
