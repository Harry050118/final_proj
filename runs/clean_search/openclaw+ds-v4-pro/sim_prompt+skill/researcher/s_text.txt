## Related Work

**LLMs for Research.** Recent work has explored the use of large language models across multiple stages of the research pipeline. Si et al. conducted a large-scale controlled human study with over 100 NLP researchers, finding that LLM-generated research ideas were judged as more novel than human expert ideas [1]. IdeaBench introduced a benchmark for systematically evaluating LLM research idea generation capabilities across novelty, feasibility, and other quality dimensions [2]. For literature synthesis, Wang et al. proposed AutoSurvey, a framework that automates comprehensive survey writing through retrieval-augmented outline generation and multi-phase refinement [3]. In the context of ML experimentation, Huang et al. introduced MLAgentBench, a suite of 13 tasks that evaluate LLM agents on designing, executing, and iterating on machine learning experiments [4]. The AI Scientist by Lu et al. demonstrated a fully automated end-to-end scientific discovery pipeline, encompassing idea generation, code writing, experiment execution, and paper composition [5]. Despite these advances, prompt-driven pipelines often struggle to produce ideas that are simultaneously diverse and practical, motivating the target paper's move toward an iterative self-rewarding framework that enables continuous refinement of research outputs through reinforcement learning.

**LLMs for Science Discovery.** The vision of AI-assisted scientific discovery has a long history, with early work in areas such as chemistry, biology, and materials science. A recent comprehensive survey by Chen et al. provides a taxonomy of AI4Research tasks spanning scientific comprehension, discovery, academic writing, and peer review, highlighting the growing breadth of AI applications across the research lifecycle [6]. Ifargan et al. proposed data-to-paper, an automation platform that guides interacting LLM agents through a complete research process from annotated data to human-verifiable manuscripts, demonstrating that autonomous cycles can produce manuscripts that recapitulate peer-reviewed publications for simple research goals [7]. A key challenge across these systems is enabling AI to move beyond passive data analysis and actively generate novel research hypotheses, which requires advances in both reasoning and creative capabilities.

**Automated Evaluation of Research Papers.** The role of AI in scientific peer review has attracted growing attention. Robertson conducted a pilot study demonstrating that GPT-4-generated reviews can provide moderately helpful feedback in the peer-review process, though with limitations in depth and specificity [8]. More broadly, RewardBench by Lambert et al. provides a systematic benchmark for evaluating reward models across chat, reasoning, and safety domains, revealing systematic shortcomings in reasoning and out-of-distribution generalization [9]. Zhang et al. proposed Generative Verifiers (GenRM), which train reward models as next-token predictors to support chain-of-thought reasoning and scalable test-time verification, outperforming discriminative reward models and LLM-as-a-Judge approaches [10]. On the application side, Yu et al. introduced Paper SEA, a structured automated peer reviewing framework that standardizes heterogeneous reviews, generates constructive feedback, and evaluates review-paper consistency through a self-correction strategy [11]. However, even advanced LLMs acting as judges still lag behind reward models specifically trained for evaluation tasks, highlighting the challenge of achieving human-level judgment and reasoning in AI-driven peer review. This motivates the target paper's approach of training a generative reward model to simulate comprehensive, multi-perspective peer reviews within an iterative research-review-refinement cycle.
