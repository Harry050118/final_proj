**Related Work**

**VLM-Based Mobile GUI Agents.** Vision-language models (VLMs) have enabled substantial progress in autonomous mobile GUI agents that interact with smartphone interfaces through visual perception and action prediction. CogAgent [1] introduced an 18B-parameter VLM with high-resolution screen encoding, achieving strong performance on the AITW benchmark by directly interpreting screenshots without relying on HTML or accessibility trees. Auto-UI [2] proposed a multimodal chain-of-action approach that fuses historical action sequences with future action plans for screen-level interaction, bypassing application-specific APIs. The Chain-of-Action-Thought (CoAT) paradigm, introduced alongside the AITZ dataset [3], further enhanced GUI agents by capturing semantic reasoning through screen context, action thinking, target specification, and outcome modeling within a multi-turn thought process. More recently, foundation action models such as OS-Atlas [4] and UI-TARS [5] have scaled GUI agents through large-scale cross-platform pretraining: OS-Atlas provides a unified grounding corpus spanning desktop, mobile, and web platforms, while UI-TARS integrates perception, System-2 reasoning, and unified action modeling into an end-to-end native agent capable of iterative self-improvement from online interaction traces. UGround [6] demonstrated that purely visual grounding, trained on a large-scale synthetic dataset of GUI screenshots, can match or exceed agents that rely on text-based interface representations. On the evaluation side, the AndroidControl dataset [7] introduced a diverse benchmark with both high-level and low-level task instructions, revealing that fine-tuned models scale effectively in-domain but exhibit slower out-of-domain generalization. Despite these advances, most existing agents are trained on static demonstration data, which limits their ability to generalize across diverse reasoning patterns and user instructions.

**Self-Training with Process-Level Rewards.** Self-training has emerged as a key paradigm for improving language model reasoning beyond supervised fine-tuning on fixed datasets. Early self-training approaches filtered model-generated responses by final-answer correctness for iterative training, but such outcome-level supervision overlooks the quality of intermediate reasoning steps. ReST-MCTS* [8] addressed this by integrating process reward guidance with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), inferring per-step rewards through tree-search rollouts to generate higher-quality reasoning traces without manual step-level annotation. Similarly, Xie et al. [9] leveraged MCTS to decompose instance-level rewards into granular step-level preference signals and applied Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) for iterative policy improvement, demonstrating that on-policy data collection is essential for stable self-improvement. These MCTS-based approaches demonstrate that tree search can provide effective process-level supervision for self-training. However, they have primarily been developed and evaluated in text-based reasoning domains such as mathematical problem solving. Extending self-training with process-level feedback to multi-modal GUI domains introduces additional challenges, including the cost of trajectory collection on real devices or simulators and the need for domain-specific reward design that accounts for both semantic reasoning and spatial grounding.

**Preference Optimization for Step-Level Reasoning.** Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [10] reformulated RLHF as a closed-form binary classification objective, enabling stable policy alignment without explicit reward model training. Building on DPO, several works have extended preference optimization to fine-grained reasoning steps. Chain of Preference Optimization (CPO) [11] constructs step-level preference pairs from tree-of-thought search trees, where reasoning steps along optimal paths serve as preferred responses and pruned branches as dispreferred ones, enabling chain-of-thought models to internalize search-quality reasoning with low inference latency. These methods demonstrate that step-level preference signals can improve reasoning quality beyond outcome-level alignment. However, they rely on the availability of structured search trees with clear win/lose branching, which is non-trivial to construct in multimodal GUI domains where action correctness depends on both semantic reasoning and spatial grounding. This motivates the target paper's approach of constructing thinking-level preference pairs from a CoaT-tree using rule-based reward backpropagation, tailored to the multi-turn reasoning structure of GUI agents.
