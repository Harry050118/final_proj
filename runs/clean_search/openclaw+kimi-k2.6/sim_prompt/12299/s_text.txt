**VLM-based Mobile GUI Agents.** Vision-language models (VLMs) have become the dominant backbone for mobile GUI agents, enabling end-to-end perception and action generation from raw screenshots. Early work such as CogAgent [1] introduced specialized visual encoders for high-resolution screenshot understanding, while SeeClick [2] demonstrated that GUI grounding pre-training directly improves downstream agent performance across mobile, desktop, and web platforms. More recently, OS-ATLAS [3] and UI-TARS [4] scaled this direction through large-scale continual pre-training on millions of GUI elements, achieving strong in-domain performance and serving as open-source alternatives to commercial VLMs. These foundation models establish the perceptual base upon which reasoning-centric post-training methods are built.

**Chain-of-Action-Thought Reasoning for GUI Control.** Beyond perception, structured reasoning has proven critical for long-horizon GUI tasks. Android in the Zoo (AITZ) [5] formalized the Chain-of-Action-Thought (CoaT) paradigm for mobile agents, decomposing each action into description, thought, decision, and grounding stages. This multi-step reasoning pattern improves interpretability and reduces error accumulation in trajectory execution. Complementary datasets such as Android in the Wild (AITW) [6] and AMEX [7] provide large-scale human demonstrations with diverse instructions and action spaces, enabling supervised fine-tuning of CoaT-capable agents. Nevertheless, the scarcity of diverse CoaT trajectories and the risk of overfitting to static reasoning templates remain open challenges.

**Self-Training and Preference Optimization for Reasoning.** To mitigate data scarcity, self-training methods iteratively generate and filter model outputs to construct new training signals. In the reasoning domain, Self-Training with Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [8] showed that preference pairs derived from correct versus incorrect chain-of-thought outputs can boost mathematical reasoning without human annotations. Iterative Reasoning Preference Optimization [9] extended this idea by repeatedly sampling reasoning paths and retraining with a composite DPO and negative log-likelihood loss. Chain of Preference Optimization (CPO) [10] and TPO [11] further explored tree-structured preference learning, constructing multi-step or multi-branch preference trees to capture finer-grained reasoning quality. These methods highlight the promise of preference-based learning for reasoning, though they have primarily been studied in text-based domains such as math and code.

**Process Reward and Tree-Search Methods.** A parallel line of work seeks to improve reasoning through step-level supervision. Process reward models (PRMs) [12, 13] assign per-step feedback, but their training typically requires expensive manual annotation of intermediate reasoning steps. ReST-MCTS* [14] addressed this bottleneck by using Monte Carlo Tree Search to infer process rewards from final-answer correctness, enabling self-training without explicit step labels. More recently, Mulberry [15] applied collective Monte Carlo Tree Search to multimodal reasoning, demonstrating that tree-based exploration can generate high-quality training data for vision-language models. These approaches share the intuition that structured search and backward credit assignment can substitute for costly process annotations.

**Reinforcement Learning for GUI Agents.** Reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a practical alternative to supervised fine-tuning for GUI agents. ARPO [16] introduced experience-replay-augmented GRPO for end-to-end policy optimization in sparse-reward GUI environments, while MobileGUI-RL [17] explored online RL with curriculum task synthesis in real mobile settings. CRAFT-GUI [18] combined curriculum learning with fine-grained reward modeling to handle task difficulty heterogeneity. These RL methods improve agent adaptability but often rely on trajectory-level rewards and do not explicitly optimize intermediate reasoning steps within the CoaT structure.

**Data Diversity and Instruction Evolution.** Data diversity is widely recognized as a key factor in preventing overfitting during fine-tuning. In the GUI domain, prior work has investigated the scaling properties of UI control data [19], showing that in-domain performance improves with data volume while out-of-domain generalization remains challenging. Instruction evolution strategies—generating varied question-answer pairs grounded in real UI screenshots—have been adopted to expand the effective training space and enhance layout understanding. Such techniques complement self-training by ensuring that the policy model explores a broad distribution of reasoning contexts rather than memorizing static trajectories.
