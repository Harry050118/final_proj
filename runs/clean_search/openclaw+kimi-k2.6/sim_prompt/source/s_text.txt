**Benchmarks and Environments for Evaluating Digital and Embodied Agents.**
The development of autonomous agents that interact with graphical user interfaces has motivated a growing family of benchmarks designed to measure open-ended task completion in realistic settings. WebArena [1] introduced a self-hosted web environment spanning e-commerce, social forums, and content management, evaluating agents on functional correctness rather than action matching. VisualWebArena [2] extended this paradigm to visually grounded web tasks, showing that vision-language models can outperform text-only agents yet still fall far short of human performance. For desktop environments, OSWorld [3] provided the first scalable, executable benchmark for cross-platform computer use, revealing substantial gaps in GUI grounding and multi-application workflows. In robotics, robomimic [4] offers long-horizon manipulation tasks with human demonstrations, serving as a standard for evaluating visuomotor policies such as Diffusion Policy [5]. Together, these benchmarks establish the diverse, multimodal settings in which verifier quality directly affects measured agent progress.

**LLM-as-a-Judge and Biases in Automated Evaluation.**
Using large language models as evaluators has become a widespread strategy for scaling assessment of generated text and agent trajectories, but a body of work has documented systematic weaknesses in this paradigm. Studies of position bias [6] and scoring bias [7] have shown that LLM judges are sensitive to prompt ordering, rubric phrasing, and reference framing, producing inconsistent ratings under seemingly neutral perturbations. Self-preference bias [8] and self-bias in self-refinement [9] demonstrate that models favor their own outputs or rationalize their earlier judgments, undermining reliability when the same model acts as both generator and evaluator. Humans or LLMs as the Judge [10] further quantified multiple judgment biases in both human and automated raters, highlighting that neither is immune to systematic skew. These findings motivate careful design of verifier prompts and metrics, especially when evaluations inform downstream training or agent steering.

**Reward Modeling and Verification for Agent Trajectories.**
Beyond general LLM judging, recent work has targeted reward modeling and verification specifically for agent behavior. AgentRewardBench [11] provided the first comprehensive benchmark for evaluating LLM judges on web-agent trajectories, revealing that rule-based methods systematically underestimate success and that no single LLM dominates across environments. GUI-Shepherd [12] introduced a process reward model trained on human-annotated GUI interactions, showing that dense, step-level feedback improves long-horizon reinforcement learning over sparse outcome rewards. Agentic reward modeling [13] proposed integrating human preference signals with verifiable correctness checks via specialized modules, aiming to reduce bias in reward construction. UI-Genie [14] and MagicGUI-RMS [15] explored self-improving GUI agents driven by reward models that verify trajectory outcomes and generate synthetic training data, while STEVE [16] used automated step verification with pre- and post-action screenshots to train computer-use agents without human annotation. These approaches share the goal of producing reliable, scalable rewards for agent improvement, yet they do not directly address the over-validation tendency that can arise when MLLMs evaluate trajectories in open-ended domains.

**Test-Time Scaling, Self-Improvement, and Online Supervision.**
A complementary line of research investigates how to improve agent performance at inference or training time by leveraging feedback signals. Reflexion [17] showed that language agents can use verbal self-reflection stored in episodic memory to boost decision-making without gradient updates, establishing a template for iterative self-improvement. ReAct [18] interleaved reasoning and acting to ground language models in external environments, providing a foundation for agent architectures that later work extended with reflection and search. Test-time scaling [19] demonstrated that allocating additional compute during inference—through iterative revision or search against a verifier—can outperform simply enlarging model parameters, underscoring the importance of verifier quality for effective scaling. Co-evolution of policy and internal reward [20] proposed self-generated reward signals that guide both inference and training, showing gains on interactive tasks. These methods highlight that the utility of feedback depends critically on the verifier’s ability to detect failures and provide balanced guidance, a requirement that remains challenging when verifiers themselves are prone to systematic bias.
