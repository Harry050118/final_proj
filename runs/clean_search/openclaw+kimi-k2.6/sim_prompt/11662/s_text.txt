**Verifiers and Reward Models for Agent Learning.**
Verifiers that assign rewards to agent behavior have been central to advances in AI, from game-playing systems guided by outcome-based rewards to large reasoning models trained with formal verifiers in code and math. In open-ended domains such as web navigation, desktop automation, and robotics, where success criteria are nuanced and multimodal, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have emerged as promising general-purpose verifiers. Prior work has explored MLLMs in evaluative roles through benchmarks such as Agent-RewardBench [1], which evaluates reward modeling across perception, planning, and safety in real-world multimodal agents, and through agentic verification frameworks such as VAGEN [2], which proactively interacts with GUI environments to overcome partial observability. Process-level supervision has also been studied for GUI agents: GUI-Shepherd [3] trains a process reward model to provide dense, step-by-step feedback, while ProgRM [4] introduces progress rewards estimated via longest-common-subsequence-based self-annotation to address sparse feedback in GUI reinforcement learning. These efforts highlight the potential of MLLM-based rewards, yet also reveal challenges in ensuring reliable verification when criteria are open-ended.

**Biases and Limitations of LLM and MLLM Judges.**
A growing body of work has documented systematic biases in LLM-as-a-Judge settings. Position bias, where judgments are influenced by the order in which candidates are presented, has been studied extensively [5]. Self-preference bias, in which models favor their own outputs, has been shown to correlate with self-recognition capabilities [6] and with perplexity-based familiarity [7]. More broadly, human and LLM judges exhibit multiple judgment biases, including leniency and authority effects, that can be exploited to manipulate evaluations [8]. Recent work has sought to disentangle true self-preference from confounds due to evaluator uncertainty on difficult queries [9], and to mitigate self-preference through structured multi-dimensional evaluation [10]. These biases are particularly concerning when MLLM verifiers are used for failure detection, online supervision, and self-improvement, where skewed judgments can propagate into training signals.

**Self-Improvement and Online Supervision with Verifier Feedback.**
Beyond offline evaluation, verifiers are used to steer and improve agents during or after execution. Reflexion [11] introduced a framework in which language agents reflect on task feedback, store verbal summaries in episodic memory, and use them to guide future decisions without fine-tuning. However, the effectiveness of such self-critiquing depends heavily on the quality of the verifier. Empirical studies have found that LLM-based self-verification can suffer from high false-positive rates, diminishing rather than improving plan quality [12]. In online settings, reinforcement learning has been applied to train GUI agents with trajectory-aware rewards [13], and process reward models have been explored to provide denser feedback than sparse outcome rewards [14]. Implicit process reward modeling [15] further enables online updates without expensive step-level annotations. These lines of work underscore that the utility of a verifier is determined not only by aggregate accuracy but also by its calibration and its ability to correctly identify failures.

**Benchmarks for Multimodal Agents and Evaluation.**
The development of MLLM verifiers has been shaped by benchmarks that demand multimodal reasoning in interactive environments. VisualWebArena [16] evaluates agents on visually grounded web tasks requiring both image and text understanding. OSWorld [17] provides a scalable, real-computer environment for open-ended tasks across operating systems, revealing large gaps between agent and human performance. Complementary benchmarks such as Windows Agent Arena [18] and stress-testing frameworks [19] further probe robustness under realistic variability. These environments serve as critical testbeds for assessing whether MLLM verifiers can generalize across diverse tasks and modalities, and whether their judgments align with human intent.
