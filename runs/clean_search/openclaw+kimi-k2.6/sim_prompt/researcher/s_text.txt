**LLMs for Research and Scientific Discovery.** Recent advances in large language models have prompted growing interest in automating scientific research. The AI Scientist introduced a fully automated, prompt-driven pipeline for open-ended machine learning discovery, generating ideas, executing experiments, and writing papers [1]. Building on this direction, multi-agent frameworks have been proposed to coordinate the full research lifecycle through modular collaboration, spanning literature review, experimentation, and peer review [2]. Complementary efforts have explored automated survey generation, where retrieval-augmented pipelines synthesize comprehensive literature reviews from large corpora [3], and multi-agent dialogue designs that improve research ideation through iterative critique and revision loops [4]. Benchmarks such as MLAgentBench evaluate language agents on machine learning experimentation tasks, revealing gaps in long-horizon planning and generalization to novel research settings [5]. Other work has examined LLM-driven idea generation through structured profiling and quantitative scoring frameworks [6]. Despite these advances, prompt-based and single-pass methods often struggle to produce ideas that are both diverse and practically grounded, motivating iterative self-improvement mechanisms that continuously refine research outputs through feedback [7].

**AI for Science and Autonomous Discovery.** The use of artificial intelligence to accelerate scientific discovery has a long history across chemistry, biology, materials science, and mathematics. Early applications treated AI primarily as a passive analytical tool for data processing within single domains [8]. More recent surveys have charted the evolution from task-specific automation toward autonomous scientific agents capable of planning, experimentation, and iterative refinement [9]. A unified framework for agentic science identifies core capabilities including planning, tool use, memory, and collaboration as essential for full research agency [10]. These perspectives highlight the broader challenge of shifting AI from a supporting analytical role to an active participant in generating new scientific knowledge, which demands advanced reasoning, creativity, and the ability to operate across the complete discovery loop.

**Automated Evaluation of Research Papers and LLM-as-a-Judge.** The application of AI to scientific evaluation has attracted significant attention, including automated summarization, error detection, and fairness analysis in peer review. Pilot studies have investigated the effectiveness of general-purpose LLMs in assisting peer review, finding that while generated reviews can achieve comparable surface-level helpfulness, they exhibit higher variance and limited sensitivity to subtle content changes [11]. Other work has examined whether ChatGPT can evaluate research quality according to established academic criteria, revealing weak individual correlation with expert judgments and limitations in fine-grained assessment [12]. Systematic efforts to standardize and evaluate automated reviewing have introduced structured frameworks for generating constructive feedback and measuring content consistency [13]. The broader LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm has been formalized through taxonomies of evaluation scope, reasoning mechanism, and reliability challenges such as consistency and bias [14]. Benchmarks like JudgeBench reveal that even advanced models struggle to distinguish factually correct responses from subtly flawed ones [15]. Generative reward models trained via next-token prediction have been proposed as an alternative to discriminative scoring, leveraging chain-of-thought reasoning to improve verification accuracy [16]. These developments underscore the difficulty of achieving reliable, human-level judgment in automated evaluation, motivating the development of specialized generative reward models trained specifically for the peer review task.
