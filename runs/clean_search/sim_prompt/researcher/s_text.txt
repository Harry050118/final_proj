**LLMs for Automated Scientific Research.** The use of large language models (LLMs) to automate or assist scientific research has attracted considerable attention. Luo et al. [1] present the first systematic survey mapping LLM applications across the full research cycle, including hypothesis discovery, experiment planning, scientific writing, and peer review. The AI Scientist [2] introduces a fully automated, prompt-driven pipeline where frontier LLMs generate research ideas, write and execute code, produce experimental results, compose full papers, and perform simulated peer review—all at low computational cost. MLR-Copilot [3] proposes a three-phase LLM-agent framework combining IdeaAgent for hypothesis generation, ExperimentAgent for implementation, and iterative debugging with human feedback. Despite these advances, most existing systems rely on commercial, closed-source models as backends, which limits reproducibility and prevents the use of reinforcement learning (RL)-based policy optimization for iterative improvement. Si et al. [4] conduct the first large-scale human study comparing LLM-generated and human-expert research ideas, finding that LLM ideas are rated as more novel but slightly less feasible, while also exposing critical limitations in LLM self-evaluation and generation diversity. These findings motivate the need for trainable, open-source models that can be refined through iterative feedback.

**LLMs for Automated Peer Review.** A parallel line of work investigates whether LLMs can assist or automate the peer review process. Liu and Shah [5] provide an early exploratory study of GPT-4 for reviewing tasks, finding that LLMs show promise for error detection and checklist verification but struggle with holistic quality judgments. D'Arcy et al. [6] propose MARG, a multi-agent review generation framework that distributes paper text across specialized LLM instances, enabling full-text processing beyond context-length limits and producing more specific, helpful feedback. Yu et al. [7] introduce SEA, a standardized framework for automated peer review that normalizes heterogeneous reviews and incorporates self-correction via mismatch scoring. Tyser et al. [8] develop AI-driven review systems with bias-aware design, meta-prompting, and role-playing strategies, evaluating alignment with human preferences through pairwise comparisons. More recently, Zeng et al. [9] apply reinforcement learning to train review models with composite reward functions combining review quality and rating consistency, achieving improved factual grounding and analytical depth. Broader surveys on LLM-as-a-Judge [10, 11] provide comprehensive taxonomies of using LLMs for evaluation, highlighting challenges in consistency, bias, and domain generalization. However, these approaches typically treat peer review as a standalone task rather than integrating it into a closed-loop system where review feedback directly drives iterative improvement of research output.

**Preference Optimization for LLM Alignment.** Aligning LLMs with human preferences has been a major focus of recent research. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) traditionally requires training a separate reward model followed by policy optimization using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [12] simplifies this by reparameterizing the reward function, enabling direct policy learning from preference pairs via a classification loss. Building on DPO, SimPO [13] introduces a reference-free reward based on the average log probability of sequences, eliminating the need for a reference model and improving alignment performance. Self-Rewarding Language Models [14] propose an iterative framework where the LLM acts as its own judge, generating preference data for DPO training and improving both instruction-following and self-evaluation capabilities across iterations. Dong et al. [15] present a practical workflow for online iterative RLHF using proxy preference models, demonstrating that iterative policy updates with freshly sampled data outperform offline approaches. Our work extends these ideas to the domain of scientific research by constructing a self-contained iterative loop where a reviewer model provides preference signals to optimize a researcher model, analogous to but distinct from general-purpose self-rewarding frameworks.

**LLM-Assisted Scientific Writing.** Beyond the full research pipeline, LLMs have also been applied to specific writing tasks in science. AutoSurvey [16] automates comprehensive literature survey generation through a multi-LLM pipeline with retrieval-augmented generation, addressing context-length limitations and factual hallucination risks. The broader survey by Luo et al. [1] systematically reviews LLM applications in scientific writing, including citation generation, abstract composition, and full-paper drafting. Our work differs by training models to generate complete research papers with structured outlines, simulated experimental results, and LaTeX formatting, conditioned on a corpus of references and their abstracts.

**AI-Generated Text Detection.** As LLM-generated content becomes increasingly prevalent, detecting machine-written text—particularly in academic settings—has emerged as a critical safeguard. Bao et al. [17] propose Fast-DetectGPT, an efficient zero-shot detector based on conditional probability curvature that distinguishes human and machine-authored text without requiring training data or watermarking. This approach achieves substantial improvements in both accuracy and computational efficiency over prior perturbation-based methods such as DetectGPT [18]. We adopt Fast-DetectGPT as a component of our ethical safeguards to ensure transparency regarding the provenance of AI-generated research outputs.
